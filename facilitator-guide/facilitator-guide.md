# Facilitator Guide — Strategic AI Leadership for HR

## Delivery Philosophy

This workshop is not a lecture series. It is a working session where senior HR professionals build things they take away and use. Every module has an exercise. Every exercise produces a deliverable. The facilitator's job is to set up the thinking, provide the frameworks, and then get out of the way while participants work.

**Tone:** Confident, practical, no jargon. "Rock and roll, not royal protocol" (Nico Decock's chairing style at AI Experience Live 2026). Challenge the room. Name the elephants. Do not sanitise the difficult questions — job losses, cost, failure rates, vendor self-interest.

**Style model:** The Arnold Clark training approach — real tasks, real documents, no artificial exercises. "Nothing this week is technical. The craft is knowing how to give clear, specific, purposeful instructions."

---

## Room Setup

### In-Person (Preferred)
- **Cabaret/rounds** — tables of 4-5 for small group work
- **Screen/projector** — for slides and live demos
- **Whiteboard or flip charts** — one per table
- **Wi-Fi** — reliable, tested before the day
- **Power** — enough sockets for Day 2 laptops
- **Printed workbooks** — one per participant
- **Sticky notes and markers** — for exercises

### Virtual Adaptation (3 Half-Days)
- **Platform:** Teams or Zoom with breakout rooms
- **Digital whiteboard:** Miro or similar
- **Workbook:** Digital PDF, pre-distributed
- **Timing:** 3 x 3.5-hour sessions with breaks

## Module-by-Module Facilitator Notes

### Module 1 — The AI Landscape

**Key risk:** The room already thinks they know this. Your job is to surprise them with the data they have not seen — especially the failure rates (80% fail to scale), the rework rate (37% of saved time), and the cognitive risk stats.

**Open with:** "Raise your hand if your organisation is using AI. Now raise your hand if you can tell me what it's worth." (Expect most hands up for the first, few for the second.)

**Slide to build:** A single slide with the five stats that cut through noise. Let participants react before you frame them.

**Exercise tip:** The reality check works best when tables compare scores honestly. Push for candour — "This is a diagnostic, not a report card."

---

### Module 2 — Workforce Architecture

**Key risk:** This is abstract for people who live in operational HR. Ground it immediately with the stat: "~90% say their job description doesn't reflect their actual work." That makes it concrete.

**The pivot:** When you introduce the 5-level fluency model, ask participants to self-assess. Most will be L1-L2. That gap between where they are and L4-L5 is the architecture challenge.

**Exercise tip:** The architecture audit is hard. Give clear instructions: pick ONE process, map it in three columns (human work / machine work / decision layer), then identify what is redundant. Tables of 4-5 working on different processes creates rich cross-table sharing.

---

### Module 3 — Adoption, Culture and Trust

**Key risk:** This module can become a complaint session about resistant colleagues. Redirect to leadership accountability: "The fire triangle says leadership is the spark. If adoption is low, that's a leadership signal, not a people problem."

**Strong moment:** The Vattenfall/KAIRO contrast. One organisation spent budget on a polished L&D programme. The other spent nothing — one person posted internally and 400 people showed up. Let the room sit with that.

**Exercise tip:** The culture diagnostic gets honest fast. Give permission: "Score your own organisation, not the one you wish you had."

---

### Module 4 — Governance

**Key risk:** Eyes glaze at "governance." Open with the brakes analogy and the lawless agent story (the AI that hires a contractor with no background check and pays them out-of-band). That wakes the room up.

**Exercise tip:** The one-page governance framework should genuinely be one page. Push back on complexity. "If your team cannot follow it without reading a manual, it will not work."

---

### Module 5 — Building the Business Case

**Key risk:** Participants build theoretical ROI models. Push for specificity: "Name the tool. Name the cost. Name the saving. Name the time period. If you can't, you don't have a business case yet."

**The worked example:** Walk through the Teradata example (50 licences x £360 = £18k → £324k value) slowly. Let participants challenge the assumptions. That challenge is the skill.

**Exercise tip:** Presentations to the room with peer challenge are essential. The board will be harder than this room — practise here.

---

### Module 6 — The Agentic Shift

**Key risk:** This can feel futuristic and irrelevant. Anchor it immediately: "Agent task success has gone from 16% to 66%. This is not theoretical. This is arriving while you're in this room."

**Demo:** If possible, show a live agent interaction (Copilot Studio, a simple workflow agent). Seeing is believing.

**Exercise tip:** The agent map needs guardrails emphasis. For each process, the "what guardrails are non-negotiable" column is the most important one.

---

### Module 7 — AI Tools for HR Leaders

**Key risk:** This is the module most likely to feel like "training." Frame it as leadership credibility: "You don't need to be a power user. You need to know enough to tell when someone is using AI well and when they're not."

**Live demos:** Run them yourself, narrating your decisions. Show a bad prompt and a good prompt side by side (the Arnold Clark CLARITY model). Let participants see the difference in real time.

**Exercise tip:** "The leader's challenge" — each participant works on their own real task. The room critique ("is the output trustworthy?") is where the learning happens. Do not skip it.

---

### Module 8 — Councils, Champions and Communities

**Key risk:** Participants design elaborate structures that will never be implemented. Push for simplicity: "What is the smallest thing you could do in the first 30 days?"

**Strong contrast:** The NTT elected council (formal, structured, cross-functional) vs the KAIRO grassroots community (organic, no budget, viral). Both work. The question is which fits your culture.

---

### Module 9 — The Honesty Conversation

**Key risk:** This is emotionally charged. People in the room may be worried about their own jobs. Create safety: "This is the conversation most organisations are avoiding. Having it here, with peers, is practice for having it with your teams."

**Do not sanitise:** Acknowledge that AI probably does mean smaller teams in some areas and radically different roles in others. The question is not whether things change — it is how honestly and humanely the organisation manages the change.

**Exercise tip:** The team conversation script is personal. Give people space to draft individually before sharing in pairs. Do not force full-room sharing unless participants volunteer.

---

### Module 10 — The 90-Day Plan

**Key risk:** Rushed, generic plans. Protect the full 90 minutes. This is the deliverable that makes the workshop worth the investment.

**Structure:** 30 minutes building individually, 30 minutes peer challenge in pairs, 30 minutes sharing key commitments with the room.

**Close with:** "In 90 days, you will be back in this room (virtually). Come with results, not excuses. Name your commitment now."

## Adaptation Notes

### For a Single Organisation
- Replace generic examples with their data, processes, and challenges
- Pre-interview 2-3 participants to tailor the exercises
- Invite their CIO/CTO for 20 minutes in Module 8 (cross-functional partnership)
- The 90-day plan can be a collective organisational plan, not just individual

### For an Open Cohort
- Use the diversity of organisations as a strength — cross-pollination of ideas
- Exercises work on individual organisational challenges but with peer input from different sectors
- Consider a follow-up community for the cohort

### For 1-Day Condensed Version
- Combine Modules 1+2 (landscape + architecture), 3+4 (culture + governance), 6+7 (agents + tools)
- Drop Module 9 (honesty conversation — it needs space)
- Shorten Module 10 to 45 minutes
- This sacrifices depth but maintains the strategic arc
